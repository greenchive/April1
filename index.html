RhythmX AI Unveils Keystroke Intent Engine, Predicts Which AI Model to Deploy Before the Question Is Asked

RhythmX AI has announced the release of its Keystroke Intent Engine (KIE), a new ambient intelligence layer that analyzes typing pressure, speed, time of day, and local weather conditions to determine which AI model should answer a clinician's question — before they finish typing it. As one early pilot physician described it, "I hadn't even hit enter yet and it already knew I needed cardiology, not general medicine. I think it also knew I was hungry."

How It Works

The Keystroke Intent Engine continuously monitors typing dynamics through a proprietary sensor layer embedded in the clinician's workflow. By measuring keystroke force in grams-force, inter-key latency at the microsecond level, and correlating these signals with circadian rhythm data, local barometric pressure, and a real-time weather feed, KIE constructs a multidimensional intent profile that determines whether a question requires the precision of a specialist model, the breadth of a generalist model, or — in rare cases documented during overnight shifts — simply a snack.

"We found a direct correlation between aggressive spacebar usage during a thunderstorm and the need for cardiology-specific responses," said Nathan, VP of AI at RhythmX. "Once we added humidity as a feature, our model selection accuracy jumped from 71% to what our team is calling 'vibes-based precision.'"

— Nathan, VP of AI, RhythmX AI

The system also factors in what the team calls "typing sentiment." A clinician hammering keys at 3 AM in a rainstorm receives a different model configuration than someone gently tapping during a sunny Tuesday lunch break. Internal testing revealed that physicians typing with one hand while holding coffee triggered a dedicated "caffeinated reasoning" pathway that skipped small talk and went straight to differential diagnosis.

What Clinicians Aren't Typing

Early pilot users responded with what the research team characterized as "stunned pauses followed by slower, more deliberate typing" — which the KIE system interpreted as deep satisfaction. One hospitalist reportedly stared at the screen for 14 seconds without pressing a key, and the system auto-generated a wellness check-in and suggested she take a walk.

A cardiologist at a pilot site noted that the system correctly identified his frustration with a formulary question based solely on the fact that he typed the word "why" using 3.2x his average keystroke force. "I didn't even finish the sentence," he said. "It already pulled up the prior authorization form. I've never felt so understood by software."

RhythmX reports that KIE will be available in staging by Q3 2026, pending a minor recalibration after the system briefly routed all queries to a poetry model during a site-wide power surge in February.
